## 1. Summary

**Title:** Statistical Learning from Attribution Sets  
**Authors:** Lorne Applebaum, Robert Busa-Fekete, August Y. Chen, Claudio Gentile, Tomer Koren, Aryan Mokhtari (Google Research, Cornell, Tel Aviv University, UT Austin)  
**Abstract:** Trains conversion predictors when each conversion links only to a **set** of candidate clicks (privacy APIs / broken deterministic joins) rather than a unique responsible click. Attribution sets are generated by an oblivious adversary with a known prior \(\pi\) over positions of the true positive in each set. The authors construct an **unbiased** population risk estimator and prove ERM generalization with sample complexity inflated by \(1/\|\pi\|_2^2\), with extensions to estimated \(\hat\pi\).

**Key contributions:**
- Theorem 1: unbiased loss estimator via affine decomposition \(\ell(h(x),y)=f_1(h(x))+y f_2(h(x))\) and combinatorial mapping from latent moments to observable set indicators (weights \(\beta_0,\beta_1\) with Binomial tails).
- Theorems 2–4: generalization and regret under ERM; robustness bounds when \(\pi\) is mis-estimated.
- Experiments on **MNIST** (1-vs-rest), **CIFAR-10** (animal vs machine), **Higgs** (binary): **UNBIASED** vs **RANDOM** and **MAX PRIOR** hallucinated-label baselines across attribution set sizes \(k\).

**Methodology:** Batch learning from streams of clicks + conversions with obfuscated linkage; ERM on global unbiased surrogate \(\hat\ell_M\); minibatch estimates for \(p\), \(\mathbb{E}[f_1],\mathbb{E}[f_2]\) in practice.

**Main results:** As \(k\) grows, RANDOM/MAX PRIOR often collapse toward trivial majority-class predictors; UNBIASED degrades more gracefully and is relatively stable under **overlapping** sets when \(pk>1\) (MNIST regime in paper).

## 2. Experiment Critique

**Design:** Toy vision/binary tasks as stand-ins for clicks—valid for theory stress-test, not end-to-end ad logs.

**Statistical validity:** Theory-first; **independence of delays \((D_X,D_Y)\) from \((X,Y)\)** is a core modeling assumption for identifiability; minibatch plug-in estimates add slight bias (authors argue negligible empirically).

**Online experiments (if any):** Not specified in source.

**Reproducibility:** Public datasets; synthetic attribution-set protocol described.

**Overall:** Strong for **privacy-era CVR training** under coarse linkage—not a path-Shapley **fractional credit** allocator for Phase 1.

---

## 3. Industry Contribution

**Deployability:** Fits Privacy Sandbox–style reporting where only candidate sets appear; needs reliable \(\pi\) or \(\hat\pi\).

**Problems solved:** Unbiased **risk estimation** for click→conversion prediction without deterministic per-click labels.

**Engineering cost:** Implementing unbiased minibatch loss + prior calibration in production streams.

---

## 4. Novelty vs. Prior Work

Contrasts with MIL/LLP (more informative bags), streaming delayed-feedback bandits (different label censorship story), and classical attribution heuristics; positions relative to large CVR/MTA experimental literature (cited list in paper).

## Project Relevance

**Phase 1 fractional labels / continuous engagement:** The core theory is built for **binary** \(Y\) and affine-decomposable losses; authors note **fractional labels** as a natural alternative to hallucinated binaries but **do not** develop them. **Multi-touch** (multiple contributing clicks) and **joint learning of \(\pi\) and the predictor** are flagged as **future work**. **Selection bias** (“active users get more touches”) is **not** the target; instead, the paper assumes **(delays ⊥ features/outcomes)**—which can be violated when activity intensity drives both touch rate and retention.

**Practical role for the survey:** Important **upstream** piece for **training predictors under privacy obfuscation**, adjacent to—not substituting for—path-level causal MTA label generation for continuous engagement.
