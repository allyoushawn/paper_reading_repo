**NotebookLM extraction (Query 1 — scope: this source):**

**(1) Core problem and key contribution**

*   **Core Problem:** In mobile advertising, publishers increasingly offer "incentivized advertising," where users are rewarded (e.g., with in-game items) for watching a video ad. However, this format suffers from an endemic issue of **adverse selection**: reward-seeking users deliberately self-select into watching these ads merely to obtain the reward, without having any genuine interest in the advertised product. This non-random assignment confounds causal inference and makes it difficult to estimate the true effectiveness of the ads.
*   **Key Contribution:** The authors develop an endogenous treatment effect model that explicitly allows and controls for **unobserved adverse selection**. By jointly modeling ad selection, intermediate clicks, and final installs, they find that incentivized ads have an overall positive effect, increasing a user's likelihood to convert (install) by 27%. They also identify a novel countervailing negative effect: users who are "averse to delayed rewards" are actually *less* likely to click on the ad because they prefer to collect their in-game reward immediately rather than taking the time to visit the App Store.

**(2) Proposed method or architecture in detail**

The proposed framework is an endogenous treatment effect model consisting of three interdependent non-linear equations:
1.  **Selection Equation:** Models whether a user selects into the treatment of watching an incentivized ad.
2.  **Intermediate Outcome Equation:** Models whether the user expresses an intention to install by clicking the ad.
3.  **Final Outcome Equation:** Models whether the user actually installs the advertised app, conditional on having clicked the ad in the intermediate stage.

To capture unobserved adverse selection, the model assumes the idiosyncratic error terms across these three equations are correlated. 

*   **Copula-based Joint Distribution:** The authors note that using a standard multivariate Gaussian distribution to model this correlation is computationally intractable for impression-level data because computing its Cumulative Distribution Function (CDF) requires slow multi-dimensional numerical integration. To achieve scalability, they assume the marginal errors follow standard logistic distributions and tie them together using a **Clayton copula**. This allows the log-likelihood function to be derived in a fast, closed-form equation.
*   **Scalable Bayesian Estimation:** Using the closed-form log-likelihood and its gradients, the model is estimated using a highly efficient Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) algorithm called the **Metropolis-adjusted Langevin algorithm (MALA)**. 
*   **Plausibly Exogenous Instrument:** To aid identification, the authors use "Device Volume" as a plausibly exogenous instrumental variable, applying a near-zero prior. The rationale is that pre-set device volume affects a user's likelihood to select into an ad due to annoyance, but does not heavily affect their final install decision since volume can be adjusted during the ad.

**(3) Datasets used for evaluation and comparison baselines**

*   **Datasets used for evaluation:** The model was estimated on a proprietary impression-level dataset from an Android mobile gaming app classified in the "Action" genre. The dataset contains 365,847 ad serving impressions generated by 143,280 unique users throughout May 2016. It tracks user characteristics (language, country, connection type, device volume, Android version, etc.) alongside their exposure to either incentivized or non-incentivized ads and their subsequent click and install behaviors. 
*   **Comparison baselines:** 
    *   **Propensity Score Methods:** The authors benchmarked their model against a two-step propensity score method (using Probit regression to estimate the propensity of treatment, followed by a control function approach with polynomial terms). This acts as a baseline that controls *only* for observable selection bias, as opposed to the proposed model which controls for unobserved selection.
    *   **Naive Probit Regressions:** The authors also provided baseline probit regressions that evaluate the treatment effect directly without controlling for any selection bias at all.

## 2. Experiment Critique

**NotebookLM extraction (Query 2 — scope: this source):**

**(1) Key quantitative results and improvements over baselines**

*   **Positive Overall Treatment Effect on Installs:** The model demonstrates that incentivized advertising increases a user's likelihood to install the advertised app by **27%** (an Average Treatment Effect of 0.000795 over a baseline of 0.00292). For the publisher, this large magnitude translates to an equivalent **increase in CPM (revenue per thousand impressions) of $0.413**.
*   **Heterogeneous Effects by Audience:** The treatment effect varies significantly depending on the user's demographic characteristics. The effect is highest for users with a Chinese device language setting (a **47% increase**, or $0.718 CPM), and lowest for Russian language users (a **13% increase**, or $0.203 CPM).
*   **Correction of Baseline Overestimation:** When benchmarking the model against a standard propensity score method (which only controls for *observable* selection bias), the baseline method severely overestimated the treatment effect on installs, predicting an ATE of 0.00187. The proposed model successfully corrected this upward bias by explicitly capturing *unobserved* adverse selection.
*   **Quantifying the Cost of Adverse Selection:** By running a counterfactual that removed unobserved adverse selection, the authors found that this bias—where reward-seeking users self-select into ads without intent to convert—costs the publisher **$0.287 in equivalent CPM**.

**(2) Limitations, failure modes, or negative results noted by the authors**

*   **Negative Result on Clicks (Intermediate Outcome):** A major negative result is that rewarding users actually *decreases* their likelihood of clicking the ad to visit the App Store by roughly 50% (an ATE of -0.0635). The authors theorize this occurs because some users are **"averse to delayed rewards"**; they prefer to collect their in-game reward immediately rather than delaying it by browsing the App Store.
*   **Failure Mode of Gaussian Distributions for Big Data:** The authors noted that modeling the dependence of unobserved errors using a standard multivariate Gaussian distribution was computationally intractable ("too slow") for impression-level data. Calculating the CDF required multi-dimensional numerical integration, forcing them to instead use a Copula-based approach (Clayton copula) to achieve a scalable, closed-form log-likelihood.
*   **Failure of Standard Observational Baselines:** Standard propensity score methods fail to accurately measure the effect in this environment. Because they rely on the "ignorability of treatment" assumption, they are invalid in the presence of unobserved reward-seeking behaviors and produce upwardly biased estimates.
*   **Lack of a Pure Exogenous Instrument:** The authors noted it was not clear *a priori* whether they possessed a truly exogenous instrumental variable within their dataset. They were forced to rely on a "plausibly exogenous" approach, using "Device Volume" as the instrument and setting a near-zero prior to allow it to have a minor effect on the outcome equation.

**(3) Top 5–7 most heavily cited prior works named in the related work or introduction**

1.  **Wooldridge  – *Econometric analysis of cross section and panel data*:** Heavily cited as the foundational textbook for standard approaches to handling treatment endogeneity in binary outcome models, as well as the baseline control function and inverse probability weighting approaches used for the propensity score methods [22, 25-29].
2.  **Evans and Schwab  – *Finishing high school and starting college...*:** Cited as a well-known prior study that estimated an endogenous treatment variable model with an instrumental variable exclusion restriction as a bivariate probit model.
3.  **Conley, Hansen, and Rossi  – *Plausibly exogenous*:** Cited to justify the paper's identification strategy, specifically using a plausibly exogenous instrumental variable (Device Volume) with a near-zero prior. 
4.  **Consumer Behavior Literature (e.g., Calder and Sternthal , MacKenzie and Lutz , MacKenzie et al. , Mitchell and Olson ):** This cluster of literature is heavily cited together to support the authors' underlying theory that a consumer's affective state and feelings of favorability toward an ad (induced by receiving a reward) mediate advertising effectiveness and increase purchase intent.
5.  **Rosenbaum and Rubin  – *The central role of the propensity score...*:** Cited for establishing the "ignorability of treatment" (selection on observables) assumption that underpins the baseline propensity score methods.
6.  **Copula Marketing Literature (e.g., Danaher and Smith [2011a,b], George and Jensen , Kumar, Zhang, and Luo ):** Cited to establish the precedent of using copulas in marketing to model the dependence among random variables, justifying their departure from Gaussian distributions.

## Project Relevance

**Low project relevance.** Based on the provided source, here is the evaluation of the paper against your project framing:

**(A) Does this paper propose or estimate per-touchpoint / per-impression / per-user incremental or causal credit that could serve as MTA-style training labels?**
No, this paper does not output a per-touchpoint, per-impression, or per-user fractional credit that can serve as supervised training labels for a multi-touch attribution (MTA) system. 

While the underlying dataset and probability calculations operate strictly at the **per-impression** granularity, the final outputs of the model are aggregated. The framework estimates population-level or segment-level **Average Treatment Effects (ATE)** by calculating the counterfactual conversion probabilities for each impression and then averaging them across the dataset. It evaluates a single binary treatment (incentivized vs. non-incentivized ads) rather than distributing fractional attribution credit across a sequence of heterogeneous touchpoints.

**(B) If not, what outputs does it actually produce and the closest connection—if any—to multi-touch attribution.**
Instead of fractional credit labels, the system produces **aggregate and segment-level causal lift metrics** and **revenue (CPM) impact estimates**.
*   **Outputs:** The model calculates the ATE of the treatment on an intermediate outcome (clicking) and a final outcome (installing). It also produces **segment-level lift** (Local ATE) based on user demographics like language (e.g., finding the largest positive treatment effect for Chinese language users and the lowest for Russian users). Finally, it translates these causal effects and the impact of adverse selection into equivalent changes in publisher CPM.
*   **Connection to MTA:** The strongest connection to your MTA project is the paper's method for resolving **unobserved selection bias**. In MTA, a persistent problem is that users with high intrinsic intent naturally accumulate more ad touches. This paper tackles a similar confounding issue ("adverse selection," where reward-seeking users self-select into ad views despite having low actual intent). It solves this by jointly modeling the selection stage, click stage, and install stage using an **endogenous treatment effect model** tied together with a **Clayton copula**. This mathematically separates the unobserved user intent from the true causal lift of the ad, which is a highly relevant concept for isolating true incrementality in an MTA system.

**(C) Applicability caveats for continuous non-purchase conversions and selection bias among active users.**
*   **Continuous non-purchase conversions:** This framework is explicitly designed for **binary outcomes**, specifically whether a user clicked (1 or 0) and whether they installed the app (1 or 0). The equations utilize logistic threshold crossing models to determine these binary states. It is not directly applicable to continuous engagement outcomes like "user days-active" without fundamentally reformulating the outcome equations. 
*   **Selection bias among active users:** While the paper's core contribution is handling selection bias, it models selection strictly as an isolated, impression-by-impression decision (a user choosing to watch a single incentivized video to get an immediate reward). It does not track long-term longitudinal engagement, nor does it address the sequential accumulation of multiple touches over time. In fact, the median user in their dataset only generated 1 impression, and the average was 2.55 impressions. Therefore, the framework lacks mechanisms to handle confounding driven by highly active users accumulating dozens of overlapping heterogeneous touchpoints over an extended lifecycle.
