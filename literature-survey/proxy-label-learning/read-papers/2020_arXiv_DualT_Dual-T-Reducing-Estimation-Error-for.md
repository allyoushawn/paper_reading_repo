# Dual T: Reducing Estimation Error for Transition Matrix in Label-noise Learning (Han et al., NeurIPS 2020)

**Authors:** Not specified in source.
**Affiliation:** Not specified in source.

## Contribution, method, and experimental setup (NLM Q1)

**(1) Core problem and key contribution**

*   **Core Problem:** State-of-the-art optical-lattice clocks (OLCs) have reached performances at or near the standard quantum limit (SQL), which arises from the quantum noise associated with discrete measurement outcomes of uncorrelated atoms [1, 2]. While systems operating at microwave frequencies have bypassed the SQL using quantum entanglement (spin squeezing), **generating entanglement on an optical-clock transition to operate a clock beyond the SQL had never been demonstrated** [1, 3].
*   **Key Contribution:** The authors successfully report the **first creation of a many-atom entangled state on an optical transition** and demonstrate an OLC operating with an Allan deviation below the SQL [1, 3]. They achieved a **metrological gain of $4.4^{+0.6}_{-0.4}$ dB over the SQL** [1, 4]. This improvement allows the clock to reach a target stability $2.8 \pm 0.3$ times faster than a standard, unentangled clock [4, 5].

**(2) Proposed method or architecture in detail**

The proposed architecture relies on an ensemble of approximately 350 $^{171}\text{Yb}$ atoms trapped in a two-dimensional "magic-wavelength" optical lattice placed inside a high-finesse optical cavity [6, 7]. The atoms are Raman sideband cooled to minimize motional interference [6, 8]. The method to create the entangled clock operates via the following sequence:
*   **Ground-State Spin Squeezing:** Because it is difficult to robustly create squeezing directly on the ultra-narrow optical-clock transition, the authors first generate a Squeezed Spin State (SSS) between two nuclear sublevels ($|↑\rangle$ and $|↓\rangle$) of the electronic ground state ($^1S_0$) [6]. They optically pump the atoms into a Coherent Spin State (CSS) using a radiofrequency (RF) $\pi/2$ pulse, and then apply a laser pulse near the $|↑\rangle \rightarrow |^3P_1\rangle$ transition through the cavity [9]. This atom-light interaction is amplified by the cavity to create an effective one-axis twisting Hamiltonian that squeezes the quantum noise into a specific spin quadrature [9, 10].
*   **State Mapping:** The resulting SSS is phase-coherently mapped onto the optical-clock transition ($^1S_0 \rightarrow ^3P_0$) by applying an optical $\pi$ pulse from the clock laser (the local oscillator, or LO), which transfers the population from the ground state $|↑\rangle$ to the excited clock state $|e\rangle$ [6, 9, 11].
*   **Squeezed Clock Operation:** The system evolves in a Ramsey sequence on this optical transition for a designated dark time $\tau_R$ [12, 13].
*   **Measurement:** A final $\pi$ pulse maps the state back to the ground-state manifold, where a state measurement of $S_z$ is performed [12, 13]. This is done by detecting the vacuum Rabi splitting of the cavity mode to determine the final spin populations [14, 15].

**(3) Datasets used for evaluation and comparison baselines**

*   **Datasets:** Because this is an experimental quantum physics paper, **it does not use any standard external datasets**. The evaluation data consists of direct experimental measurements generated by the physical system, such as Rabi spectroscopy of the clock transition, Ramsey fringes, and self-comparison Allan deviation measurements over time [11, 16-18].
*   **Comparison Baselines:**
    *   *The Standard Quantum Limit (SQL):* The primary theoretical baseline is the SQL for a perfect clock operating with uncorrelated atoms ($N=300$) and perfect state detection, representing the limit for classical independent-particle metrology [2, 19].
    *   *Coherent Spin State (CSS):* The primary experimental baseline is the exact same optical clock apparatus operated using an **unentangled Coherent Spin State (CSS)** instead of the Squeezed Spin State (SSS) [13, 18, 20]. The authors directly compare the Allan deviation of the SSS clock sequence (R1) against the CSS clock sequence (C1) under identical physical conditions [17, 18].

## Results, limitations, and prior work (NLM Q2)

**(1) Key quantitative results and improvements over baselines**

*   **Metrological Gain over the SQL:** The authors successfully demonstrated an Optical-Lattice Clock (OLC) utilizing a many-atom entangled state, achieving a metrological gain of $4.4^{+0.6}_{-0.4}$ dB over the Standard Quantum Limit (SQL) [1, 2].
*   **Averaging Time Reduction:** This entanglement allowed the squeezed clock to reach a given stability $2.8 \pm 0.3$ times faster than an ideal clock operating exactly at the SQL [2].
*   **Allan Deviation:** The squeezed clock demonstrated an Allan deviation of $\sigma_{sq} = (1.30 \pm 0.04) \times 10^{-13} \text{ s}^{1/2}/\sqrt{T}$ [3]. For comparison, the theoretical SQL for a perfect clock operating with an unentangled Coherent Spin State (CSS) under identical parameters is $\sigma_{SQL} = 2.1 \times 10^{-13} \text{ s}^{1/2}/\sqrt{T}$ [3].
*   **Improvement Over the Actual Baseline:** Compared to the actual experimental CSS baseline—which suffers from imperfect state detection and contrast loss—the squeezed clock achieved a larger improvement of **5.7 dB**, which translates to a factor of 3.7 reduction in averaging time [3].
*   **Spin Noise Reduction:** State tomography revealed that the squeezed noise dipped significantly below the SQL to $\xi^2 = -5.9^{+0.6}_{-0.8}$ dB, which successfully survived being mapped onto the clock transition and back [4].

**(2) Limitations, failure modes, or negative results noted by the authors**

*   **State Detection Noise:** The authors note that the achievable metrological gain in their system is heavily restricted by state detection noise, which accounts for nearly half of the total detected spin variance [5]. This limited the observable spin noise suppression to 6 dB instead of the intrinsic 9 dB [6].
*   **Entanglement Lifetime:** While the system's overall ensemble spin coherence decays exponentially with a time constant of $\tau_{ens} = 0.8 \pm 0.2$ s, the Squeezed Spin State (SSS) is no longer sufficiently entangled to overcome the SQL after an interaction time of approximately **0.2 seconds** [7, 8]. It can, however, still offer gain over a classical CSS for up to 0.5 s [8].
*   **Trap Light Scattering:** The excited clock state $|e\rangle$ has a limited lifetime of about 800 ms due to the scattering of trapping light, which causes the spin noise to slowly increase over time [5, 9]. 
*   **Local Oscillator (LO) Phase Noise:** The observed Ramsey contrast decays rapidly with a short time constant of $\tau_{LO} = 6$ ms strictly due to the phase noise of the Local Oscillator laser [10]. 

**(3) Top 5–7 most heavily cited prior works named in the related work or introduction**

1.  **Braverman et al. (2019):** Heavily cited as the direct foundation for this experiment, having demonstrated the near-unitary spin squeezing between ground-state Zeeman sublevels of $^{171}\text{Yb}$ that the authors utilize [11, 12].
2.  **Wineland et al. (1994):** Cited to establish the "Wineland parameter" ($\xi_W^2$), which the authors use throughout the paper to mathematically quantify variance reduction, ideal clock limits, and metrological gain [13-15].
3.  **State-of-the-Art Optical Clocks (e.g., Ludlow et al., 2015; Ushijima et al., 2015; Oelker et al., 2019):** Cited collectively in the introduction to establish that current optical atomic clocks have reached fractional stabilities at the $10^{-18}$ level, operating near or at the SQL limit [13, 16].
4.  **Kitagawa and Ueda (1993):** Cited as the theoretical foundation for "Squeezed Spin States" and the one-axis twisting Hamiltonian utilized by the authors [6, 17, 18].
5.  **Leroux et al. (2010):** Cited for previous demonstrations of squeezing the collective spin of a dilute atomic ensemble via cavity feedback, as well as orientation-dependent entanglement lifetimes [19-21].
